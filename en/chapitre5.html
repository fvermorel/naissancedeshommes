The olive tree
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
When Cecrops died, Craneos ascended to the throne of Athens. The town grew and became more beautiful, and more and more powerful. Two gods were interested in it and fought over its protection: Athena, the Goddess of Wisdom, and Poseidon, the God of the Sea. Poseidon offered the horse to the residents, and Athena gave them the olive tree.
The king and his subjects preferred the offer of Athena. The olive tree would allow them to eat, in times of war and peace. They named their city “Athens”, and adopted the owl, the sacred animal of Athena, as their emblem.
La culture de l’olivier est très importante en Grèce et sur tout le bassin méditerranéen : l’huile est un aliment fondamental.
Orpheus
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
Orpheus was the son of the muse Calliope. He was the greatest musician ever seen on earth: his song was so beautiful that even bears, lions, and panthers would cry when hearing him, and would become completely harmless. It was even said that he could move rocks and stones.
He was the favorite of the god Apollo, who had offered him a lyre to which two strings had been added, so there were nine, as many as there were muses. Orpheus was a traveler who never stayed in one place, finding his inspiration as he went. With Jason, he participated in the quest for the Golden Fleece, and his adventures are told in our book Jason and the Argonauts.
Descent to Hell
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
Orpheus fell in love with Eurydice, a wood nymph. She was incredibly pretty and he loved her madly. Every day, he would write the most beautiful songs to glorify his fiancée. Eurydice did not resist long and she accepted his proposal. Alas! The day of their wedding, a viper bit Eurydice, who died instantaneously. Orpheus was inconsolable.
Apollo requested a favor from his uncle, Hades, who, because he loved his nephew, agreed to make an exception. He would release Eurydice from the Kingdom of the Dead, but with one condition: Orpheus had to find her himself and bring her back to the surface; and, during the entire return trip, he would not be allowed to look behind him or help his spouse.
Return from Hell
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
Orpheus obeyed and went to get Eurydice. But how difficult the voyage was, coming back from the land of the Dead...
During the whole trip, Orpheus, in distress, heard the complaints and cries of Eurydice, who suffered horribly. They were nearly at the exit when Eurydice became quiet. Not a word, not a noise. Orpheus took fright and turned around: woe! Eurydice disappeared.
Orpheus went back to Earth and began to cry and cry and cry… He was inconsolable. The Bacchantes could no longer bear it. They cut him up in pieces and threw him into the sea. But his severed head continued to sing: it is that head that taught humans all the rules of music.
L’histoire tragique d’Orphée et d’Eurydice a fait l’objet de nombreuses pièces de théâtre, opéras ou films...
Asclepius
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
Asclepius was the son of Apollo and the princess Coronis. He was brought up by the centaur Chiron, who taught him medicine. He became an incredible healer, capable of curing the most serious diseases, undoing the most deadly venoms, sewing up the most horrible wounds. But one day, he created a potion capable of resuscitating the dead.
Zeus could not accept someone thus defying the order of the world, and struck down Asclepius with lightning. But he was quickly overtaken with remorse. The doctor had only acted out of love for humanity. Zeus had changed, and did not want to harm mankind further. So, he turned him into a constellation. From that time, Asclepius became the god of Doctors.
J’ai mal à la tête. J’ai mal aux pieds. Mon bébé a mal aux dents. Mon ventre s'agite.
Le bâton d’Asclépios, entouré de serpents, sert aujourd’hui d’emblème à certaines pharmacie.
Conclusion
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
Well, well, have we travelled far! Before, we were just meek creatures, naked and defenseless. We were fearful of everything: of Zeus, of animals, and even of our own shadows. Now, we live in great cities. We know how to cultivate the earth in order to eat. We know how to fix our wounds and our sicknesses. We know how to sing and to make music. And finally, most importantly, we know that we must not irritate the gods.
But life is not easy: war, old age, sickness, disease, and, at the end of the path, always death, which is the fate no one can escape. But regardless, hope forces us to move forward; hope forces us always to try to create a better world.
